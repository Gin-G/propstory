### Why "total snowfall" overstates the *skiable base* here
1. **Semi-arid + very sunny → sublimation and rapid melt.** In the arid West, snow disappears by **sublimating directly to vapor**, and abundant sunshine drives melt. South-facing aspects can melt out repeatedly mid-winter.
2. **High year-to-year variability.** Spring 2026 was Colorado's **worst snowpack on record — ~22% of the April-1 median, with Kremmling specifically cited.** A "ski backyard" thesis lives or dies on the *bad* years, not the average.
3. **Aspect matters more than the address.** North-facing slopes hold snow far longer than south/west faces. The viability of your "backyard run" depends heavily on which way the skiable terrain faces.

## 2. Is it windy? — The valley reads calm; a ridgetop will not be

- The **town station** reports gentle averages (~4–6 mph; windiest month June ~6 mph). **Do not trust this for a ridgetop.** Valley anemometers badly understate exposed-ridge wind, and Middle Park / North Park are regionally known for wind.
- **This is the core problem for your thesis.** On a ridgeline, wind **scours snow off the windward face down to bare/thin ground** and **deposits it on the leeward side** as drifts and wind slab. So a ridgetop "backyard":
  - **Windward/top:** thin, crusty, inconsistent — poor and intermittent base.
  - **Leeward:** deep drifts, but **wind-slab / cornice / avalanche concern** and uneven coverage — not a casual ski-out-the-door surface.
- Net effect: even in a good snow year, a **ridgetop tends to be the worst place on the mountain to retain an even, skiable base.** The snow that falls there often ends up somewhere else.

## 5. Investment read against your own test

Your test is exactly the right one: **"Can it build a skiable base, reliably?"**

- **If the value you're paying for is backyard skiing:** the evidence points to **poor fit** — modest snowfall, semi-arid sunny rapid melt, high variability, and (most importantly) **ridgetop wind scouring** that prevents an even base. You'd likely get **opportunistic powder turns after storms**, not a dependable season. Paying a premium *for the skiing* would be **speculative.**
- **If the value is land / views / big-sky recreation:** a ridgetop can be a great buy for exactly the opposite reasons (views, exposure, privacy, hunting/hiking access near public land). Just **decouple** that value from the ski thesis.

### Before making an offer — verify (in priority order)
1. **Aspect & pitch of the "ski" terrain** (county GIS + a slope/aspect map). North-facing + sustained pitch = the difference between viable and not.
2. **Multi-year winter satellite imagery** (Google Earth Pro / Sentinel) of the parcel — does snow actually persist?
3. **On-site visit in mid-winter** — feel the wind, probe the snow depth, look for scoured ground and flagged trees.
4. **Talk to neighbors / the HOA** about typical winter snow depth and wind.
5. **Nearest SNOTEL** depth/SWE history for the real local trend (and how bad the bad years get).
6. **Confirm listing specs** (price, acreage, access/road maintenance in winter, water/well, septic).
